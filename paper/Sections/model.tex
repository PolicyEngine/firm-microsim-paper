\section{A Calibrated Notch-Bunching Model of Firm Turnover}
\label{sec:model}

The reduced-form evidence in Section~\ref{sec:bunching} establishes that firms
respond to the VAT registration notch, but it cannot price reforms the data
never observed---a relocated threshold, a reduced small-firm rate, or a tapered
schedule. To do that I calibrate a notch-bunching model of firm turnover,
following the framework of \citet{klevenwaseem2013}, \citet{saez2010}, and
\citet{kleven2016}, as applied to UK VAT by \citet{liuetal2021}. The UK
registration threshold is a \emph{notch}, not a kink: once turnover crosses the
threshold the firm becomes liable for VAT on its \emph{entire} turnover, not
merely on the increment above it. I model this notch exactly. The behavioural
elasticity that governs the response, and its calibration, are reported in
Section~\ref{sec:calibration}.

This is a calibrated policy model, not a structural identification of firm
primitives. I assign each firm a cost/productivity parameter consistent with
its observed turnover and inputs and re-solve its problem under counterfactual
schedules; I do \emph{not} claim to recover a policy-invariant productivity
distribution or to identify structural primitives. Papers that do identify such
primitives at a size threshold---\citet{garicano2016} and
\citet{gourioroys2014}---instrument or structurally restrict the technology; I
differ precisely by not making that claim.

\paragraph{Scope: a turnover-tax notch.} The model taxes \emph{turnover} through
a one-input technology. Real VAT is levied on \emph{value added} with input-tax
reclaim, and roughly $45\%$ of below-threshold firms register voluntarily to
recover input VAT \citep{liuetal2021}. My model abstracts from input reclaim and
voluntary registration: it is a turnover-tax-notch approximation. This is a scope
limitation I state up front; it bounds the welfare claims I make and is
revisited in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}.

\subsection{The firm's problem with a VAT notch}

Each firm $i$ chooses turnover $y_i$ to maximise after-tax profit. Let $c_i(y_i)$
denote the cost of producing turnover $y_i$, $T^{*}$ the registration threshold,
and $\tau_{\max}$ the standard VAT rate. Because VAT applies to the firm's entire
turnover once it registers, after-tax revenue is discontinuous and profit is
\begin{equation}
  \pi_i(y_i) \;=\;
  \begin{cases}
    \;y_i - c_i(y_i), & y_i < T^{*} \quad \text{(unregistered)},\\[4pt]
    \;(1-\tau_{\max})\,y_i - c_i(y_i), & y_i \ge T^{*} \quad \text{(registered)}.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:firm-problem}
\end{equation}
At $T^{*}$ after-tax revenue falls discretely by $\tau_{\max}\,T^{*}$: a firm
that nudges its turnover from just below to just above the threshold loses
$\tau_{\max}\,T^{*}$ of net revenue on turnover it was already earning (about
$\pounds17{,}000$ at my parameters). This discrete drop---absent from a kink, where
only the \emph{marginal} rate changes---is the defining feature of a notch and
the source of the bunching documented in Section~\ref{sec:bunching}.

\subsection{A Cobb--Douglas cost specification}

I specify cost through a one-input Cobb--Douglas technology relating turnover
$Y_i$ to input expenditure $X_i$,
\begin{equation}
  Y_i \;=\; A_i\, X_i^{\alpha},
  \qquad
  c_i(y_i) \;=\; \left(\frac{y_i}{A_i}\right)^{1/\alpha},
  \label{eq:production}
\end{equation}
where $A_i>0$ is a firm-specific cost/productivity parameter and $\alpha>0$ a
common curvature parameter. The implied marginal cost is
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial c_i(y_i)}{\partial y_i}
  \;=\; \frac{1}{\alpha}\,\frac{c_i(y_i)}{y_i}
  \;=\; \frac{1}{\alpha}\,\frac{X_i}{Y_i},
  \label{eq:mc}
\end{equation}
identified from the firm's input--output ratio in the data. The curvature
parameter is calibrated to $\alpha \approx 0.989$ (Section~\ref{sec:calibration}),
i.e.\ near-constant returns, so marginal cost is close to $X_i/Y_i$.

\subsection{The dominated region}
\label{ssec:dominated}

The notch creates a range of turnover just above $T^{*}$ that \emph{no} firm
chooses, because every point in it yields strictly lower profit than locating at
$T^{*}$. A firm at the threshold earns net revenue $T^{*}$; a registered firm at
$T^{*}+a$ earns $(1-\tau_{\max})(T^{*}+a)$. Equating the two (the cost difference
across this narrow band is negligible), the upper edge of the dominated region is
\begin{equation}
  (1-\tau_{\max})\,(T^{*}+a) \;=\; T^{*}
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  a \;=\; T^{*}\,\frac{\tau_{\max}}{1-\tau_{\max}} .
  \label{eq:dominated}
\end{equation}
This is the Kleven--Waseem dominated-region width \citep{klevenwaseem2013}. At
my parameters it gives $a=\pounds21{,}250$, so the dominated region is
$(T^{*},\,T^{*}+a)=(\pounds85{,}000,\ \pounds106{,}250)$. This $\sim\pounds21$k
band---an order of magnitude wider than the $\sim\pounds1$k region implied by a
smoothed \emph{kink} approximation---is the load-bearing analytic object of the
paper: it is exact, independent of $\alpha$ and of any smoothing parameter, and
it underwrites the efficiency ranking of reforms in
Section~\ref{sec:counterfactuals}. I return to the kink contrast in
Appendix~\ref{app:notch}.

\subsection{The marginal buncher}
\label{ssec:buncher}

Firms whose frictionless optimum lies above $T^{*}$ choose between
\emph{bunching} at the threshold (remaining unregistered) and \emph{registering}
beyond the dominated region. The marginal buncher $y_H$ is exactly indifferent
between the two; all firms with frictionless optimum in $(T^{*},\,y_H]$ bunch at
$T^{*}$, while firms above $y_H$ register and jump past the dominated region. The
width of the bunching segment $\Delta y^{*}=y_H-T^{*}$ is the structural
counterpart of the excess mass measured in Section~\ref{sec:bunching}, and it
pins down the behavioural turnover elasticity $e$, exactly as in
\citet{klevenwaseem2013} and \citet{saez2010}.

Because $\alpha\approx0.989$ implies near-constant returns, I evaluate the
marginal buncher in the standard Kleven--Waseem iso-elastic representation, in
which the behavioural elasticity $e$ governs the response, rather than through the
Cobb--Douglas branch optimum. This gives $y_H=\pounds127{,}382$
($\Delta y^{*}/T^{*}=0.50$) at the median elasticity $e=0.17$, rising to
$y_H=\pounds143{,}527$ at the mean elasticity $e=0.32$; in both cases $y_H$ lies
well above the dominated region, as it must. Figure~\ref{fig:notch-fit} displays
the resulting structure---a spike of bunchers at $T^{*}$, the empty dominated
region, and registered firms beyond $y_H$---against the observed turnover
distribution.

\subsection{The calibration input and forward solution}
\label{ssec:recovery}

To re-solve the firm's problem under a counterfactual schedule I need a
firm-level cost parameter consistent with each firm's observed allocation. I
take it as the production residual
\begin{equation}
  A_i \;=\; \frac{Y_i}{X_i^{\alpha}},
  \label{eq:recover}
\end{equation}
using only observed turnover $Y_i$, inputs $X_i$, and the calibrated $\alpha$.
This residual is a \emph{calibration input}, not an identified structural
primitive: it is whatever cost level rationalises the firm's observed turnover
and inputs at the $\pounds85{,}000$ schedule, and I hold it fixed when I change
the schedule. As noted above, identifying such a parameter structurally is the
contribution of \citet{garicano2016} and \citet{gourioroys2014}; I do not claim
it.

\paragraph{Forward solution.} Holding $\{A_i\}$ fixed, I re-solve each firm's
problem with the schedule replaced by a counterfactual---a relocated threshold, a
changed rate, or a tapered schedule. For this computation I represent the
schedule with a smooth (sigmoid) effective rate,
$\tau(y)=\tau_{\max}/(1+e^{-k(y-T^{*})})$, calibrated so that its steepness $k$
reproduces the dominated region of Section~\ref{ssec:dominated}
(Appendix~\ref{app:notch}). Because the near-constant-returns technology makes a
differentiable first-order condition ill-conditioned, I locate each firm's
optimum by maximising after-tax profit directly over a bounded
$\pm\pounds20{,}000$ grid around its observed turnover rather than by root-finding.
The bounded window is a modelling assumption, justified by the localisation of the
realised response (Section~\ref{sec:results_structural}); I flag that it
mechanically limits how far firms reposition. The smooth schedule and the grid
search are the \emph{computational implementation} of the notch model, not a
competing specification. These counterfactuals are developed in
Section~\ref{sec:results}; they are distinct from the reduced-form density
counterfactual introduced next.

\subsection{A reduced-form counterfactual density}

To quantify the distortion induced by the notch I also require the turnover
distribution that would obtain in its absence. Rather than re-solving the firm's
problem, this object estimates the smooth, undistorted size distribution directly
from the data, using the same polynomial counterfactual as the reduced-form
bunching estimator of Section~\ref{sec:bunching}. Measuring each observation by
its distance from the cutoff $y_{\mathrm{dist}} = T - T^{*}$, I fit a third-order
polynomial by ordinary least squares to the data outside a window
$[\,T^{*}-W,\; T^{*}+W\,]$ with $W=15$k,
\begin{equation}
  f^{\mathrm{cf}}(T) \;=\;
  \beta_0 + \beta_1\, y_{\mathrm{dist}} + \beta_2\, y_{\mathrm{dist}}^{2}
  + \beta_3\, y_{\mathrm{dist}}^{3},
  \label{eq:cf-poly}
\end{equation}
and project it back into the excluded region. The gap between the observed density
and~\eqref{eq:cf-poly}---excess mass below $T^{*}$ and missing mass above
it---measures the behavioural response, in the reduced-form tradition of
\citet{liuetal2021}; it is not the structural re-optimisation of
Section~\ref{sec:counterfactuals}.
