\section{Bunching at the VAT Threshold}
\label{sec:bunching}

This section presents reduced-form evidence of firm bunching at the VAT
registration threshold, using the polynomial counterfactual-density estimator
of \citet{saez2010} and \citet{klevenwaseem2013} as applied to UK VAT data by
\citet{liuetal2021}; I also draw on the synthesis of \citet{kleven2016} and
the friction diagnostics of \citet{chettyetal2011}. The estimator is purely
reduced-form: it fits a smooth counterfactual to the empirical turnover density
and reads excess mass off the gap, without imposing any structural model of the
firm. The notch mechanism that generates this mass---VAT applying to a
registered firm's entire turnover, so that locating just below the cutoff is
attractive---is the subject of Section~\ref{sec:model}; here it suffices that the
notch produces excess mass below the threshold and missing mass above it. I
estimate bunching at the \pounds85{,}000 threshold prevailing in my 2023--24
data.

\subsection{Methodology}

Following \citet{liuetal2021}, I group firms into fine turnover bins of
\pounds100 and estimate a counterfactual density---the distribution that would
obtain absent the registration notch---by polynomial regression on bin counts,
excluding a manipulation window around the threshold $y^{*}$:
\[
  c_j \;=\; \sum_{l=0}^{q} \beta_l \, (y_j)^{l}
  \;+\; \sum_{i \in [y^{*}_{-},\, y^{*}_{+}]} \gamma_i \, \mathbb{1}\{j = i\}
  \;+\; \varepsilon_j ,
\]
where $c_j$ is the number of firms in bin $j$, $y_j$ is the distance of bin $j$
from the threshold, $q$ is the polynomial order, and $[y^{*}_{-},\,y^{*}_{+}]$
is the excluded window. The fitted polynomial is then projected into the
excluded region to recover the counterfactual density $g(y)$. Excess bunching is
the integrated difference between the observed and counterfactual densities just
below the threshold,
\[
  B(y^{*}) \;=\; \int_{y^{*}-\Delta y^{*}}^{y^{*}} g(y)\,\mathrm{d}y
  \;\approx\; g(y^{*})\,\Delta y^{*},
\]
the approximation holding when $g$ is locally smooth. The bunching ratio
$b(y^{*}) = B(y^{*})/g(y^{*}) \approx \Delta y^{*}/y^{*}$ is the width of the
bunching region expressed as a fraction of the threshold---a normalised
\emph{ratio}, not an elasticity. Translating the excess mass into a behavioural
elasticity requires a further set of assumptions, discussed with explicit
caveats in the comparison below; the
estimator here delivers the raw geometry of the response.

\subsection{Results}

For the 2023--24 data, measured at the \pounds85{,}000 threshold, my
\emph{preferred} specification imposes the mass-conservation restriction that
excess mass below the threshold equal missing mass above it, locates the
marginal buncher endogenously, and bootstraps the sampling distribution
(Appendix~\ref{app:inference}). It yields a bunching ratio $b = 0.060$ (SE $0.005$) and excess
mass of $8{,}712$ firms (SE $662$), reproducible by \texttt{python -m bunching}
on the regenerated 2023--24 synthetic data; full inference (bootstrap standard errors and the
mass-conservation constraint) is reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:inference}. Mass
conservation matters: an unconstrained polynomial fit implies excess against
missing mass that grossly violate adding-up, whereas the constrained estimator
reconciles the two. In all
specifications the observed density displays clear excess mass in the \pounds100
bins immediately below the threshold and a corresponding deficit just above it,
the signature of a notch-induced behavioural response. A non-trivial share of
firms near the threshold thus adjust their turnover to avoid registration.

It is worth fixing at the outset that the bunching evidence speaks to
\emph{several} distinct elasticity-like objects, which the literature too often
runs together and which I keep separate throughout. (i) The \emph{notch-width
elasticity} ($\approx 0.45$, Method~B below) infers a turnover elasticity from
the empirical width of the excess-mass span in the Kleven--Waseem manner; it is
the bunching-implied elasticity I report descriptively in this section. (ii) The
\emph{marginal-buncher elasticity} ($\approx 0.66$) reads the indifference
condition of the firm at the upper edge of the bunching segment. (iii) The CES
\emph{substitution elasticity} $\sigma \approx 4.85$ is a wedge-substitution
parameter recovered from the above/below mass ratio, not a turnover-supply
elasticity at all. All three are bunching-based geometric readings of the same
excess mass; none should be read as a substitute for another, and---for the
reasons set out below---none should be read as a welfare-sufficient structural
turnover elasticity.

\subsection{Comparison with the literature}
\label{ssec:bunch-lit}

The closest comparator is \citet{liuetal2021} (hereafter LLAT), who estimate
bunching at the same \pounds85{,}000 UK VAT notch over a manipulation window of
$-\pounds14$k to $+\pounds24$k. To compare like with like I normalise my excess
mass to the LLAT-style excess-bunching statistic (excess mass per unit of
counterfactual density at the threshold), which yields $b_{\mathrm{LLAT}} = 0.94$
(SE $0.07$, $95\%$ CI $[0.80,\,1.09]$) against their $b = 1.361$ (SE $0.202$).
The two are of the same order of magnitude; my synthetic-data response is
somewhat less pronounced. This is what one would expect: my firm records are
built up from ONS business-structure counts and calibrated to aggregate HMRC
totals rather than drawn from the administrative micro-data LLAT observe, so the
near-threshold spike is smoothed by the synthesis step and by survey weighting,
and the bootstrap re-binning further attenuates the peak. The gap is therefore a
feature of the data-generating process, not evidence of a different behavioural
mechanism. Like LLAT, my setting features substantial voluntary registration
below the threshold---LLAT report that roughly $29.8\%$ of firms register
voluntarily---which dampens observed bunching relative to a pure turnover-tax
notch and which my turnover-tax-notch model abstracts from.

Several caveats govern the elasticity comparison, and I emphasise that the
bunching-implied turnover elasticity ($\approx 0.45$, Method~B, from the notch
width) is a \emph{descriptive} reading of the excess-mass geometry, not a
structural estimate I stand behind. First, LLAT \emph{deliberately decline} to
report a turnover or output elasticity, arguing that it is not point-identified
at a notch and is not a welfare-sufficient statistic; their excess-bunching ratio
is the object they stand behind, and I follow them in not treating my number as a
sufficient statistic for welfare. Second, the bunching-implied number is
attenuated and confounded by optimisation frictions: observed bunching is far
smaller than a frictionless notch model predicts, so any elasticity backed out of
the raw excess mass mixes the structural response with friction. Third, a
turnover notch confounds an output-supply response with a demand-side
(pass-through) response, so the bunching-implied number is an upper envelope on
the pure supply elasticity rather than a clean structural elasticity. My
bunching-implied elasticities---$0.45$ from the notch width (Method~B) and
$0.66$ from the marginal-buncher indifference condition---should therefore not be
read as comparable to an LLAT estimate, but against the Kleven--Waseem
structural-elasticity tradition \citep{klevenwaseem2013}, whose notch-derived
taxable-income elasticities fall below $\sim 0.3$ once optimisation frictions are
allowed for. My estimates sit somewhat above that band, consistent with the
supply-plus-pass-through confound just noted. A close
methodological analogue is \citet{almunialopezrodriguez2018}, who find Spanish
firms at an enforcement notch suppress revenue by up to $7.5\%$. The OBR figures
are corroborating order-of-magnitude context, not a like-for-like match:
\citet{obr2023vat} count firms holding turnover below the frozen threshold over a
\emph{wide} band (rising from $23{,}000$ in 2017--18 to $\sim 44{,}000$ in
2025--26, with foregone turnover of \pounds110m rising to \pounds350m), a
different object from my excess-mass spike of $\sim 8{,}700$ firms in the bins
immediately below the cutoff.

\subsection{From reduced-form evidence to structure}

These estimates quantify the aggregate response at the prevailing threshold but
do not identify the underlying firm-level productivity distribution. For a move
of the threshold itself the reduced-form counterfactual already suffices; what it
cannot price is a change to the \emph{shape} of the schedule, which has no single
point of excess mass to forward-map. What the structure of
Section~\ref{sec:model} \emph{does} deliver exactly, for every reform considered,
is the notch's dominated region and how each schedule change alters it; a fully
behavioural forward solve that re-prices each firm's turnover under counterfactual
schedules would require further parameter-sensitive assumptions and is left to
future work.
