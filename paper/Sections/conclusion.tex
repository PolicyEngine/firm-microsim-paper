\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper contributes an open, reproducible microsimulation for costing UK VAT
registration-threshold reforms, and uses it to price and \emph{rank} a menu of
reforms by efficiency. The framework---a synthetic firm dataset calibrated to HMRC
VAT aggregates, a reduced-form bunching estimator, and a calibrated
\citet{klevenwaseem2013} notch model used to re-solve firm behaviour under
alternative schedules---is released in full for replication. Its value is as
usable policy infrastructure rather than as a structural identification result: the
recovered cost parameters are a calibration input for re-solving reforms, not
claimed structural primitives, and papers such as \citet{garicano2016} and
\citet{gourioroys2014}, which do identify such primitives at a size threshold, are
the precedent I differ from precisely by not making that claim. The analysis is
anchored to the 2023--24 data year, in which the statutory threshold was
\pounds85{,}000. Table~\ref{tab:summary} collects the headline objects.

\paragraph{Findings.} First, \emph{the distortion is modest and localised}. Firms
bunch below the notch, but the headline behavioural object---the local turnover
elasticity (median $\approx 0.17$)---is small, broadly comparable to the UK
VAT-\emph{base} elasticity of \citet{liuetal2021}, though the two are distinct
objects, so the comparison is an order-of-magnitude sanity check. Only a minority
of near-threshold firms reposition, and within a narrow band.

Second, \emph{threshold-location moves are fiscally small and just relocate the
notch}. Moving the threshold is approximately linear in revenue
(Table~\ref{tab:static_revenue}) and adequately costed by a static calculation; as
a pipeline sanity check, my static costing of the real
\pounds85{,}000 to \pounds90{,}000 reform agrees with HMRC's published figures
to within \pounds1m in 2025--26. But raising the threshold does not remove the
notch's distortion---it transports the same \pounds21{,}250 dominated region to the
new cutoff at a comparable fiscal cost.

Third---and centrally---\emph{schedule reforms remove the distortion, and the taper
is the efficiency winner}. The notch creates a dominated region of width
$a=T^{*}\tau/(1-\tau)=\pounds21{,}250$, a pure misallocation zone in which no firm
optimally locates. Ranking the menu on how much of this zone each reform removes per
pound of static cost (Table~\ref{tab:efficiency}), a location move removes
essentially none; a banded reduced rate shrinks it (to \pounds9{,}444 at $10\%$);
and the graduated taper eliminates it entirely, at a static cost
(\(-\)\pounds335m) below that of the \pounds85{,}000 to \pounds100{,}000
threshold move (\(-\)\pounds371m). Schedule reforms thus dominate location moves on
efficiency, and the taper---a continuous phase-in that leaves no discrete wedge and
no secondary notch---is the most efficient option in the menu. This is a robust,
analytic deadweight/misallocation comparison based on the dominated region; the
static revenue figures are robust to the smoothing parameter, while the behavioural
re-optimised magnitudes are sensitive to it and are reported only as directional.
Because a smooth or banded schedule leaves no single point of excess mass, these
reforms are exactly what the calibrated model is needed for.

\paragraph{Limitations.} The model is a \emph{turnover-tax-notch approximation},
and this is its first-order limitation. It
treats VAT as a cost on turnover, whereas real VAT is levied on value added with
input reclaim, and roughly half of below-threshold firms register
\emph{voluntarily} because reclaim outweighs the output liability---the central fact
in \citet{liuetal2021}. The effective notch is therefore smaller (and, for
net-reclaim firms, can reverse sign) than my flat treatment implies. The microdata
are \emph{synthetic}---ONS business-structure counts calibrated to HMRC aggregates---
so agreement with HMRC totals is a consistency check, not external validation. The
analysis is \emph{partial-equilibrium}, abstracting from VAT pass-through into prices
\citep{benedek2015, belloncopestake2022}. And while the dominated-region efficiency
ranking is robust and analytic, the \emph{behavioural} revenue and distributional
magnitudes are parameter-sensitive (to the smoothing parameter $k$ and the
re-optimisation window), which is why I headline the static costs and the analytic
distortion measures rather than the re-optimised numbers.

\paragraph{Future work.} The most direct extension is a full \emph{welfare/MVPF
analysis} that models value added and input reclaim, converting the
dominated-region distortion measures into the cost--benefit terms policy needs, and
the design of \emph{revenue-neutral} variants of the taper and reduced rate that
neutralise the notch at no net fiscal cost. Modelling \emph{voluntary registration}
and the input-VAT margin explicitly would let the framework represent the full UK
VAT population rather than the consumer-facing subpopulation it currently best
describes. Allowing sector heterogeneity and linking the microdata to
\citet{policyengine} would refine both the bunching estimates and the
counterfactuals. Above all, \emph{validation against administrative firm-level data}
would replace the present internal-consistency checks with genuine out-of-sample
tests.
